# Editorial Principles

## Why This Exists

Public systems depend on measurement.

Governments allocate funding, evaluate programs, identify needs, and make policy decisions using data. Measurement is essential. Without it, large systems cannot understand scale or remain accountable.

Measurement also has limits.

Some of the most important parts of human experience resist simple quantification. Trust, dignity, healing, safety, and long-term stability rarely fit neatly within reporting periods or performance metrics. Prevention is often visible only through the absence of crisis. Recovery unfolds according to human timelines rather than institutional ones.

This project is not an argument against data.

It is an argument for reading data carefully.

Things We Measure explores the space between what can be counted and what can only be understood through context, observation, and lived experience. It does not reject quantitative evidence. It asks what becomes possible when data and lived experience are interpreted together rather than treated as competing forms of knowledge.

The goal is not certainty.

The goal is better questions.

## Our Responsibility

Every visualization makes choices.

Every interaction emphasizes certain ideas while leaving others in the background. Every annotation, transition, comparison, and definition shapes how a visitor understands the subject.

Those choices carry responsibility.

This project approaches visualization as a form of interpretation rather than neutral presentation. It seeks to be transparent about uncertainty, careful with language, and honest about the limits of available evidence.

People should never become examples used to strengthen an argument.

Complexity should not be removed simply because it is difficult to visualize.

The goal is not to eliminate ambiguity.

The goal is to represent it honestly whenever possible.

We recognize that every visualization represents people whose lives continue after the chart ends.

Absence is sometimes evidence. Silence is sometimes information.

## The Role of Data

Data makes large systems easier to see.

It helps us understand scale, identify trends, evaluate programs, allocate resources, and make decisions across systems that are too large for any one person to observe directly.

Every measurement reveals something. Every measurement leaves something outside the frame.

Every dataset is the result of choices about what will be measured, how it will be defined, when it will be collected, and how it will be grouped. Those choices make comparison and accountability possible. They also shape what remains visible and what falls outside the frame.

Recognizing these limits does not diminish the value of data.

It strengthens our responsibility to interpret it carefully.

This project treats data neither as objective truth nor as something to distrust. It is one way of understanding complex systems, not the only one. Its greatest value emerges when it is interpreted alongside lived experience, operational knowledge, and an honest acknowledgment of uncertainty.

## The Role of Storytelling

Systems are lived before they are measured.

Stories also have limits.

A single experience cannot represent every experience, just as a single metric cannot represent every person. Both can deepen understanding. Both can also narrow it when treated as complete.

This project does not use storytelling to simplify complexity or to persuade through emotion alone. It uses narrative to slow observation, provide context, and create space for questions that data cannot answer by itself.

The goal is not to replace evidence with stories.

It is to allow evidence and lived experience to remain in conversation with one another.

## Writing Principles

The writing should begin with observation before interpretation.

It should make room for complexity rather than resolving it too quickly.

Facts should carry emotional weight without being made more dramatic than they are.

Whenever possible, concrete language should be preferred over abstraction.

Questions are often more valuable than conclusions.

The writing should trust the reader.

The writing should resist easy answers.

Rather than telling visitors what to think, it should help them notice something they may not have seen before.

Interpretation should remain proportional to the available evidence.

## Visual Principles

The visual language should support understanding rather than compete for attention.

Space, typography, pacing, scale, and motion are treated as part of the argument rather than decoration.

Visual restraint is preferred over spectacle.

Silence, space, and pacing are treated as forms of communication.

Charts should reveal complexity rather than simplify it unnecessarily.

Beauty should emerge through clarity, rhythm, proportion, and thoughtful composition rather than visual excess.

## Interaction Principles

Interactions should deepen understanding rather than reward exploration for its own sake.

Motion should communicate change, uncertainty, comparison, or time.

Whenever possible, the interaction itself should carry the argument.

Visitors should experience ideas rather than simply read about them.

Every interaction should answer a meaningful question.

If an interaction does not contribute to understanding, it should not exist.

Visitors should be invited to notice rather than instructed what to believe.

## What We Will Not Do

We will not use trauma as decoration.

We will not invent stories to strengthen an argument.

We will not present uncertainty as certainty.

We will not confuse clarity with oversimplification.

We will not reduce people to examples or data points.

We will not use interaction for novelty alone.

We will not sacrifice dignity for visual impact.

## The Question We Return To

How do we measure a system without reducing the people inside it?
